# Illustrated Vault — Product Brief

Last updated: 2026-07-02

## Product vision

Illustrated Vault (illustratedvault.com) is a premium visual archive and collection companion for Pokémon card collectors.

It helps collectors browse, understand, organize, and gradually build their physical card collection around the artists, sets, cards, and artwork they care about.

## Product spine

**Beautiful visual organization + intentional collecting.**

The near-term wedge:

**Make Illustrated Vault the best artist-first Pokémon card collection experience on the web.**

The long-term ambition:

**Become the best visual archive for building a physical Pokémon card collection.**

## The questions the product answers

- What do I own?
- What am I missing?
- What do I want?
- What should I hunt next?
- What kind of collection am I building?

## Core product loop

**Discover → Want → Acquire → Archive → Share → Hunt again**

The app supports real physical collecting behavior: card shows, shop visits, trade lists, wanted lists, and binder planning.

## Core positioning

Illustrated Vault should not feel like a generic tracker, marketplace, or price app.

It should feel like:

- a curated card archive
- a personal collector vault
- a visual gallery
- a binder companion
- an artist-focused discovery tool

The desired feeling is calm, premium, taste-driven: warm archive, not game splash screen. The brand carries quiet personal mythology — a subtle ember (flame) and a subtle celestial glint (star/purple) — used sparingly and never literally or mascot-like.

## Primary user

A physical Pokémon card collector who:

- buys cards online
- trades with friends
- attends card shows
- cares about artwork and illustrators
- wants a better collection experience than a spreadsheet
- may still want pricing references, but does not want prices treated as perfect truth

## Differentiation

Most collection apps are organized around card name, set, market value, and owned/not owned.

Illustrated Vault is organized around:

- illustrators
- artwork
- visual discovery
- collection progress and intent
- sets and variants
- collector context

Artist-first browsing is a strategic strength.

## Product pillars

### 1. Visual archive

Beautiful browsing by artist, set, era, language, and artwork. Card art is the star of the UI.

### 2. Personal collection

Owned/missing states, CSV import, manual corrections, favorites, shared binder views.

### 3. Intentional collecting

Hunt intent (want / hunting / maybe / ignore) and the Hunt Board turn the missing list into an acquisition plan. Intent is planning metadata — it never changes ownership or completion.

### 4. Collector intelligence

Pricing references, eBay sold links, value confidence, last-updated context. Pricing is context for buying decisions, not the product's identity.

### 5. Curation layer

Manual corrections, artist aliases and FK identity, notable cards, enrichment of missing API data, editorial quality.

## Strategic abstraction — collection goals (long-term)

Illustrated Vault should eventually organize around **collection goals**: Artist, Set, Pokémon, Custom List, and Binder Plan are different *goal types* a collector can pursue, each with its own progress, hunt targets, and showcase.

This abstraction must be **earned gradually**. Each goal type ships as a focused, opinionated experience (Artist first, then Set Lens, then Pokémon Lens, then Custom Lists, then Binder Plans). The app must never collapse into a generic database/filter tracker — the goal model is a way to deepen intentional collecting, not to genericize the product.

## Product promise

Build a beautiful visual archive of your Pokémon card collection — organized by the artists, sets, and cards you care about.
